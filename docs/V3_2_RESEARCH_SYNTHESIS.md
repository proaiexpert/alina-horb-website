# Alina Horb Website — V3.2 Research Synthesis

Last updated: 2026-07-14

## Purpose

This document converts the July 2026 Deep Research Pack into project decisions for the four existing Ukrainian and Russian Notes articles. It is a working editorial and safety standard, not a legal opinion, medical protocol, diagnosis guide, or claim that any external professional standard automatically governs Alina's practice.

The complete research pack remains supporting material. This synthesis is the adopted implementation layer.

## Adopted conclusions

### Recommended editorial sequence

The four articles should be refined in this order:

1. **What happens during the first consultation** — clearest search intent, closest to booking, and lowest clinical-claim risk.
2. **How to begin when the request is difficult to formulate** — reduces the main emotional barrier to first contact.
3. **When familiar coping strategies stop helping** — broadest informational reach, but needs stronger safety editing.
4. **Stress, relocation, and loss of familiar support** — strongest thematic differentiation and highest contextual/safety complexity.

This order is based on editorial value, user journey, and safety risk. It is not based on commercial keyword-volume data.

### Four-level safety model

Each article should distinguish between:

1. **Educational information** — recognizable situations described without diagnosing the reader.
2. **Self-observation** — duration, intensity, worsening, effect on sleep/work/study/relationships/self-care, and whether usual support still helps.
3. **Professional support** — a calm suggestion that discussing the situation with a qualified professional may be useful when it persists, intensifies, or disrupts daily life.
4. **Urgent help** — explicit routing to local emergency or crisis services when there is immediate danger, suicide/self-harm risk, violence, inability to stay safe, or loss of contact with reality.

The website, booking form, Telegram, Instagram, and email are not emergency services.

### Core claim rules

Do not publish:

- diagnosis from a symptom list;
- universal claims that a specific number of days or weeks determines whether help is needed;
- promises that consultation will make the reader feel better;
- claims that the first consultation always follows one fixed script;
- claims of absolute confidentiality in every circumstance;
- universal migration stages or statements that relocation itself equals trauma;
- unsupported statements about Alina's methods, specialization, jurisdiction, testing, risk assessment, or clinical practice.

Preferred wording uses:

- `може / может`;
- `іноді / иногда`;
- `залежно від ситуації / в зависимости от ситуации`;
- `можна обговорити / можно обсудить`;
- `не є діагнозом / не является диагнозом`.

## Important corrections to the raw research pack

### External standards are references, not automatic law

NIMH, WHO, NHS, NICE, HCPC, and Google documentation are useful editorial references. They do not automatically define the legal or professional rules that apply to Alina. Final claims about confidentiality, informed consent, minors, couples, cross-border work, records, risk response, and scope of practice must be checked against Alina's actual status and applicable jurisdiction.

### The NIMH two-week model must not become a site rule

NIMH's public infographic uses symptom severity, duration, functioning, worsening, and immediate-risk distinctions. The site may use that general logic, but must not publish a universal rule such as “after two weeks you need a psychologist” or “before two weeks help is unnecessary.”

### Crisis wording should be contextual

A full crisis disclaimer is required on content that discusses suicide, self-harm, violence, or acute safety risk. It does not need to dominate every article. A concise sitewide boundary can be paired with a stronger article-specific notice where relevant.

### Proposed slugs are not automatically adopted

The research pack contains recommended slugs. Existing production routes should remain stable unless a route inventory and redirect plan explicitly approve changes. Avoid changing indexed or shared routes merely for wording preference.

### FAQ questions are a fact-check list, not immediate public copy

The research pack's FAQ inventory is useful, but answers must not be published until the underlying facts are confirmed. The public FAQ should remain concise and user-focused; internal confirmation questions belong in project governance.

## Article 1 — First consultation

### Primary search intent

The reader wants to know what normally happens, what may be asked, whether preparation is required, and whether the specialist/format feels suitable.

### Article objective

Reduce uncertainty without promising a fixed process or immediate outcome.

### Required content

- purpose of a first meeting;
- preparation is optional;
- possible starting questions, written with qualifiers;
- organizational questions: duration, price, payment, cancellation, contact, confidentiality boundaries;
- what the person does not have to do immediately;
- how to evaluate whether the communication and boundaries feel appropriate;
- possible next steps, only after Alina confirms her real process.

### Safe direct-answer concept

A first consultation is generally a meeting to describe the situation, discuss expectations, ask organizational questions, and understand a possible format of work. A diagnosis or perfectly formulated request is not required. Exact structure depends on the specialist and the situation.

### Required Alina confirmations

- actual first-session process;
- duration and price;
- cancellation/rescheduling terms;
- whether intake, questionnaires, or testing are used;
- how confidentiality and its limits are explained;
- whether recommendations or referrals may follow;
- whether the first session is a full paid session;
- whether the client may pause or decline further work.

## Article 2 — Difficult to formulate a request

### Primary search intent

The reader wants to know whether it is acceptable to seek support without understanding exactly what is happening or what to ask for.

### Article objective

Remove the “I must arrive with the correct psychological wording” barrier.

### Required content

- a request is not a diagnosis or exam answer;
- ways to begin with events, current difficulties, daily-life impact, or uncertainty;
- sample opening phrases;
- optional notes or chronology, without turning preparation into homework;
- permission to ask for a pause or say that a topic is difficult;
- how a request may become clearer during conversation, without promising that this occurs in one meeting.

### Avoid

- interpreting difficulty speaking as suppression, dissociation, alexithymia, resistance, attachment style, or hidden subconscious content;
- promising that the psychologist will understand without words;
- promising that a “safe space” guarantees disclosure.

## Article 3 — Familiar coping strategies no longer help

### Primary search intent

The reader wants practical orientation on when ordinary rest, self-help, or support from close people may no longer be sufficient.

### Article objective

Offer non-diagnostic orientation based on persistence, intensity, worsening, functioning, and available support.

### Required content

- difficult experiences do not automatically mean a disorder;
- look beyond one symptom;
- duration, intensity, progression, and daily-life impact;
- changed circumstances and accumulated load;
- low-pressure self-care options;
- when a professional conversation may be useful;
- when medical or urgent help is more appropriate.

### Avoid

- “five signs you urgently need a psychologist” framing;
- self-diagnosis;
- “nervous system exhaustion” as a diagnosis;
- fear-based conversion language;
- any promise that consultation will restore, heal, or guarantee relief.

## Article 4 — Stress, relocation, and loss of familiar support

### Primary search intent

The reader wants to understand why life may feel harder after voluntary or forced relocation even when immediate practical tasks are partly resolved.

### Article objective

Explain that relocation can affect routines, roles, language, relationships, belonging, safety, and material conditions without pathologizing every migrant or displaced person.

### Required content

- relocation is a cluster of changes, not one event;
- voluntary and forced relocation should not be treated as identical;
- support systems that may be lost or altered;
- why distress may become noticeable at different times;
- practical and social supports, not only individual coping;
- professional support as one option;
- clear recognition that housing, legal, medical, financial, language, violence, and safety needs may require other services.

### Avoid

- “relocation is trauma”;
- universal adaptation stages;
- claims that migration causes depression or PTSD;
- promises that psychology will restore a sense of home;
- worldwide-service claims until jurisdiction and client-location rules are confirmed.

## Cross-site editorial requirements

### Native-language workflow

- Ukrainian is the primary canonical editorial version.
- Russian is a separate natural localization, not a sentence-by-sentence translation.
- Titles, H1s, meta descriptions, examples, and anchor text should be edited independently for each language.
- Bidirectional hreflang and self-references must remain correct.

### Article structure

Each final article should include:

- direct answer in the first 80–120 words;
- category and reading time;
- visible author and confirmed credential;
- publication date and meaningful update date;
- clear H2/H3 hierarchy;
- practical self-observation or preparation block;
- educational/diagnostic boundary;
- related articles;
- contextual links to process, support areas, Notes, and contact;
- calm CTA without urgency manipulation;
- source list or editorial references where factual claims require support.

### Internal linking

Each article should normally include:

- one contextual link to the consultation process;
- one link to areas of support;
- one calm contact link;
- two related article links;
- one Notes-index link.

Use descriptive anchors rather than repeated “learn more” links.

### Author presentation

Current approved minimum:

- UA: `Аліна Горб — психолог, магістр психології`;
- RU: `Алина Горб — психолог, магистр психологии`.

Do not add titles such as psychotherapist, clinical psychologist, medical psychologist, trauma therapist, licensed psychologist, or PTSD expert without explicit evidence and approval.

### Structured data

Use only accurate, visible information:

- `Article` or `BlogPosting` for article pages;
- `BreadcrumbList` for route hierarchy;
- `Person` for Alina where the credential and profile are visible;
- `WebSite` at site level.

Do not use medical entity types that misrepresent the site or Alina's professional status.

### Dates

Change `dateModified` only after a meaningful factual or editorial update. Do not refresh dates solely to create an appearance of freshness.

## Source maintenance

For clinically sensitive or safety-relevant claims, maintain a lightweight source ledger containing:

- claim ID;
- exact claim;
- source organization and title;
- publication/update/access dates;
- article and section;
- jurisdiction or population limitations;
- status: active, review, or replace.

Preferred source order:

1. WHO and intergovernmental organizations;
2. national public-health services;
3. official clinical or professional guidance;
4. systematic reviews and meta-analyses;
5. universities and high-quality secondary sources for non-clinical context.

Do not rely on competitor SEO articles, random clinic blogs, social media, forums, or AI summaries for health claims.
